Website

The Adventurists brand is an idiosyncratic mix of the voice of Reeves & Mortimer with a broken vintage aesthetic - it’s definitely theirs and theirs alone. And, when you spend most of your life in some of the most remote places on the planet organizing the kind of antics that see 1000s of people drive some very unsuitable vehicles from London to Mongolia, one thing you’re sure to have is some pretty special photos.

All of this made designing for The Adventurists a lot of fun, and we developed a rich, textural design framework and put photography right at its heart.

One of the challenges of this project lay in structuring the site which needed to be home to 6 adventure microsites as well as having all the registration and customer management built in. When adventures are announced the places often sell out in a matter of hours so the platform needed to be robust and scalable, ready to deal with sudden surges in traffic and requests.

The adventure micro-sites make use of full bleed photography to create a more immersive feeling, evocative of that adventure, interspersed with the why and wherefores of how to take part. Elsewhere the site is a mixture of blogs and ramblings about where and what The Adventurists might be up to next - did we mention they’re currently working on a moon landing? Seriously.

Interactive Maps

When an adventure is live the adventure pages become interactive maps which track the progress of all of the teams taking place and, through the virtue of some pretty hardcore technical jiggery-pokery, teams can send sms, mms, email and video updates all of which are pinned against their journey.

The maps aggregate all of this content presenting an almost realtime stream of events as they unfold. Within this each team has its own page, detailing their own journey - how many miles they’ve travelled, how much they’ve raised for their charity, together the updates they’ve sent along the way.

When you consider that around 2500 people take part in the Mongol Rally, the behind the scenes tech to make all of this happen is the unseen hero of the project. Generally people don’t take a GPS enabled, or even a ‘modern’ phone, with them so every update goes through some hefty algorithmic mincing to determine its point of origin before it’s placed on the map.
